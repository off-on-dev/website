About OffOn

For the People Who Keep Open Source Alive

→ Sustain the people behind open source and grow the maintainers of tomorrow
→ Everyone belongs here: learners, engineers, contributors, maintainers, writers, designers, translators, organisers
→ Learn through real challenges, share what you know, help the next generation find their footing
→ Community knowledge that is preserved, passed on, and built to grow

always on. always open. always learning.

  • OffOn was started as a Dynatrace OSPO initiative. It was created to support the people behind open source and help build the open source contributors of tomorrow.
  • The community is open to everyone who cares about open source, whether you write code or not. Developers, writers, designers, translators, first-time contributors, and long-time maintainers all belong here.
  • One of the core offerings of OffOn is hands-on challenges built around real tools and real scenarios. Things you can fork, learn through doing, and build on.
  • We also want to make sure community knowledge is preserved and passed on, not lost in chat threads.
What We've Built

Adventures

Hands-on scenario-driven challenges built around real open source tools. Runs entirely in GitHub Codespaces.

→ Pick a scenario at the difficulty that fits where you are
→ Launch in Codespaces and work through real-world failures
→ Earn Credly badges and leaderboard points along the way

Play challenges

  • Adventures are monthly themed challenges, each with usually three difficulty levels so new users can learn in a structured way.
  • Everything runs in GitHub Codespaces; no local setup, no Docker, no "works on my machine."
  • Current tools: OpenTelemetry, Kyverno, OpenFeature, ArgoCD, OpenTofu, Grafana. More being built.
  • After completing an adventure, we now offer Credly badges and Linux Foundation vouchers as rewards.
  • Check out the challenges at offon.dev/challenges, or propose an adventure around an open source tool you are an expert in.

Speaking

What Makes a Good Talk

→ An intro to the open source project you maintain or contribute to
→ A demo of something you built with open source tools, including what broke
→ A concept explained clearly for a mixed audience, with a working example
→ A contribution story: your first PR, a tricky bug, a community experience

Not a good fit: product demos, vendor pitches, or talks that require a commercial tool to be relevant.
