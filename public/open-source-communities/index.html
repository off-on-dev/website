  • There is a comfortable story we tell ourselves. Projects succeed because the code is good. Ship better code, and the rest sorts itself out.

reality

What Actually Kills Projects

✷ Unclear contribution paths
✷ Expectations never written down
✷ A few absorb all the pressure
✷ Fragile, then it stalls
  • Here is what actually ends projects. Nobody knows how to contribute, so they do not.
  • Expectations are never written down, so everyone guesses.
  • A few people end up carrying all the work and all the pressure.
  • It holds for a while, then it gets fragile and stalls.
  • Next, a better way to look at this.
reframe

A Socio-Technical System

✷ A socio-technical system
✷ Code and community are one system
✷ Community design matters as much as code
  • Here is the reframe. Open source is a socio-technical system.
  • The code and the community are not two things you manage separately. They are one system.
  • So community design matters as much as the code.
  • Next, what this looks like when it goes wrong.
problem

The Maintainer Trap

✷ Work piles onto a few people
✷ The hero maintainer burns out
✷ A single point of failure is a design flaw

Example: Rust

Topic-focused teams (compiler, language, libraries, tooling, infrastructure) each own their area independently. A leadership council coordinates across teams. Ownership is spread by design, so no single person carries the whole project.

  • This is the maintainer trap. The work piles onto a few people.
  • We celebrate the hero who carries everything, and it feels great, right until it does not. That is burnout.
  • When one person becomes a single point of failure, that is a design flaw in the community, not a weakness in the person.
  • Rust is a good counter example. Instead of one or two heroes, it runs on topic focused teams. Each team owns its area and sends someone to a leadership council. Ownership is spread on purpose.
  • Next, the good news is this is fixable. Five levers.

The Fellowship

The work is too much for one person. It has to be shared.

framework

Five Levers That Decide Whether a Project Lasts

Governance

Who decides, and how. Make decision-making explicit before you need it.

Contributor Experience

How easy and rewarding it is to help. Lower the cost of showing up.

Recognition

Whether people's work is seen and valued. Most community labor is invisible.

Local Community

Where belonging actually forms. Global projects still need local homes.

Communication

Whether people can explain what it solves, and why. This is how a project gets known.

  • Here are five levers that decide whether a project lasts.
  • Governance, who decides and how. Contributor experience, how easy it is to help. Recognition, whether the work gets seen. Local community, where people feel they belong. Communication, whether people can explain the why.
  • We will walk through each one with an example of a community that does it well.
  • Next, lever one, governance.
lever 1

Governance

✷ Decide how you will decide, early
✷ Put shared functions under a neutral body
✷ Spread maintainer roles across people
✷ Give conflict a clear path

Example: Apache Software Foundation

Committees elect committers on merit. Individuals represent themselves, not employers. Decisions happen in the open: if it wasn't recorded, it didn't happen.

  • Governance is about deciding how you will decide, before you are in a crisis.
  • Put shared functions under a neutral body so they do not land on individuals. Spread maintainer roles. Give conflict a clear path.
  • Apache is a good model. Each project is run by a committee that elects committers on merit. People take part as individuals, not as their employer, so no single company can buy control.
  • Governance is boring until the day you badly need it.
  • Next, the code of conduct.
lever 1 · code of conduct

A Code of Conduct

✷ Explicit rules, not vague good vibes
✷ Name what is not acceptable: harassment, discrimination, personal attacks, sustained disruption
✷ Rules only matter if they are enforced

Example: Contributor Covenant

A widely adopted code of conduct. Clear expected behavior, clear unacceptable behavior, and a path to report and enforce.

  • A code of conduct is part of governance. It sets explicit rules, not vague good vibes.
  • It should name what is not acceptable, harassment, discrimination, personal attacks, sustained disruption.
  • And it only works if it is enforced. A document nobody acts on is worse than none.
  • The Contributor Covenant is a common starting point, adopted across many projects.
  • Next, lever two, contributor experience.
lever 2

Contributor Experience

✷ Lower the cost of a first contribution
✷ Give newcomers a clear place to start
✷ Visible path to trusted contributor
✷ If it is confusing, they quietly leave

Example: Kubernetes

Published contributor ladder: from member to approver, with clear expectations at each step. Good first issues, mentoring cohorts, and a graceful way to step back.

  • The goal is to lower the cost of a first contribution. Give newcomers a clear place to start. Make the path to trusted contributor visible.
  • Kubernetes does this well. A published ladder from member to reviewer to approver, good first issues, mentoring, and a graceful way to step back.
  • When it is confusing, people do not complain. They quietly leave, and you never find out why.

✷ 45 min just to open the issue, scared of being judged
✷ Nobody judged. Community was welcoming

drupal.org/node/3227702

  • This is my own first contribution, back in 2021.
  • I started during Covid and did not know the community yet. It took me forty five minutes just to open the issue, because I was scared of being judged.

  • Next, lever three, recognition.
lever 3

Recognition

✷ Most labor is invisible: review, triage, docs, mentoring
✷ Invisible work gets undervalued
✷ Record who did the work, sponsors included

Example: Drupal Contribution Credit System

A public record of who did what, across code and the invisible work. Contribution becomes visible instead of assumed.

  • Most of the work in a community is invisible. Review, triage, docs, mentoring. It does not show up like a shiny feature, so it gets undervalued.
  • The fix is to make it visible. Record who did the work, and let that credit reach the sponsors too.
  • Drupal does this with a contribution credit system that records it at scale.
  • Next, why that changes behavior.
lever 3

Why Recognition Changes Behavior

✷ Tie recognition to real work
✷ Companies get visible standing for what they fund
✷ It becomes routine, not a favor

When a company treats contribution as part of the actual work, with real time and budget behind it, the alignment becomes real and it lasts. Good incentive design beats good intentions every time.

  • Here is why it changes behavior. People get recognition tied to real work, not just self promotion.
  • Companies get visible standing for what they fund, so their interest lines up with the health of the community.
  • Recognition stops being a favor and becomes routine, something the system produces.
  • The pattern I keep seeing, when a company treats contribution as real work with time and budget behind it, the alignment lasts.
  • Good incentive design beats good intentions every time.
  • Next, lever four, local community.
lever 4

Local Community

✷ Global projects need local belonging
✷ Pair global events with local ones
✷ Local events lower the barrier
✷ "Come for the code, stay for the community"

Example: Drupal

DrupalCon globally, local volunteer-run camps. Small and accessible by design. That's where new people get pulled in and where most new organizers learn the ropes.

  • Even a global project needs places that feel local. Pair the big global event with small local ones. The local events are where the barrier really drops.
  • This is the one I know best. Drupal has DrupalCon globally, but the real engine is the local camps, run by volunteers, kept small on purpose. That is where new people get pulled in, and where most new organizers, including me, learn how it works.
  • There is a line I love, come for the code, stay for the community. That is a design goal, not a slogan. People commit through other people, not through repositories.
  • Next, lever five, communication.
lever 5

Communication

✷ Open source projects need people who can explain what problem it solves and why
✷ Great code that nobody understands stays unused
✷ Especially important early, to make a project known
✷ Docs, tutorials, blogs, videos, talks, case studies
  • A project is not just the code. It also needs people who can communicate around it. What problem does this solve? Why does it matter?
  • Those questions get asked by every potential contributor, every company considering adoption. If nobody answers them well, the project stays invisible.
  • This matters most at the beginning, before there is a community to spread the word.
  • The channels are many: documentation, tutorials, blog posts, videos, conference talks, case studies. Pick the ones that fit you and do them on purpose.

priorities

What to Do Now vs Later

optimize early
✷Clear contribution paths
✷Explicit, written expectations
✷Distributing authority early

Cheap now. Expensive to fix later.

safe to delay
✷Heavy formal governance
✷Complex tooling and automation
✷Over-structuring small communities

Heavy process on a tiny project can strangle it before it grows.

  • Not everything deserves attention at once.
  • Do these early, clear paths and onboarding, written expectations, and spreading authority before the load gets heavy. Cheap now, expensive later.
  • These can wait, heavy governance, complex tooling, and over structuring a community that is still small.
  • Heavy process on a tiny project can strangle it before it grows.
  • Next, a quick nuance about best practices.
nuance

Good Advice Needs the Right Partner

"Be welcoming to everyone"

Works when paired with triage, so maintainers can keep up.

"Move fast"

Works when paired with shared decision rights, so people stay aligned.

"Document everything"

Works when paired with clear ownership, so the docs stay current.

Context decides whether a practice helps. The practices aren't the problem; the pairing is.

  • The same advice can help or hurt depending on what you pair it with.
  • Be welcoming to everyone works with triage. Move fast works with shared decision rights. Document everything works with clear ownership.
  • The practices are not the problem. Pair each one with the thing that makes it work at scale.

the ai question

Help and Hazard

where ai helps
✷Lowers the barrier to a first contribution
✷Speeds up docs, translation, and issue triage
✷Could ease the "privilege of free time" open source depends on
where ai strains
✷Low-effort contributions can overwhelm reviewers
✷The cost shifts from author to maintainer
✷Access is unequal. AI can become a new privilege.

AI doesn't replace community design. It raises the stakes. Share the cost and skill. Build it into contributor experience.

  • I have to talk about AI. On the good side, it lowers the barrier to a first contribution, speeds up docs, translation, and triage, and helps people who do not write English as a first language.
  • There is a deeper promise. Dries Buytaert put it well, AI can shorten the time it takes to understand a codebase enough to act, so it could ease the privilege of free time that open source has always leaned on.
  • But access is not equal. The best tools cost money and skill. So we have to share two things, the cost, with lower cost and open models, and the skill, by building AI know how into the contributor experience.
  • The strain is real. It is cheap to generate a change, but a maintainer still has to understand it. The test is simple, does it help more people go from issue to tested patch, and make it easier to trust and merge.
  • AI does not replace community design. It raises the stakes on the same levers.

mental model

Thrive vs Decay

thriving projects
✷Share decisions
✷Make it easy to help
✷Recognize the work
✷Build local belonging
✷Communicate the why
decaying projects
✷Concentrate decisions
✷Leave contributors guessing
✷Let invisible work go unseen
✷Stay purely global
✷Stay hard to understand

The drift is slow and quiet. That's exactly why it's easy to miss.

  • If you take one thing home, take this. It maps onto the five levers.
  • Thriving projects share decisions, make it easy to help, recognize the work, and build local belonging.
  • Fading projects do the opposite. Decisions pile onto a few people, contributors are left guessing, invisible work goes unseen, and it never feels local.
  • The hard part is the drift is slow and quiet. There is no alarm, so it is easy to miss until it is far along.

What You Can Realistically Influence

You don't need to become a full-time maintainer.

✷ Document one unclear process
✷ Review a first contribution
✷ Credit invisible work out loud
✷ Help one local event

These are small structural acts. They compound.

  • You do not need to become a full time maintainer.
  • Document one unclear process. Review someones first contribution. Credit invisible work out loud where people can see it. Help one local event happen.
  • These are small acts, and they add up.
  • That is basically my story. I said yes to one event in 2023, then a few more committees, and it led to the Women in Drupal Award. No master plan, just one small yes at a time.
