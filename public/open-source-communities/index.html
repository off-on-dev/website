The Comfortable Myth

→ We assume projects succeed or fail on code quality
→ Better architecture, cleaner tests, faster releases, and the rest takes care of itself
→ That story is mostly wrong
  • There's a comfortable story we tell ourselves in open source. Projects succeed because the code is good. Better architecture, cleaner tests, faster releases, and the rest takes care of itself.
  • It's a tidy story. It's also mostly wrong.

reality

What Actually Kills Projects

→ Contribution paths are unclear, so people don't contribute
→ Expectations are implicit, never written down, so everyone guesses
→ A small group quietly absorbs all the work and all the pressure
→ Then burnout hits, and the project stalls
  • Here's what actually kills projects. Nobody knows how to contribute, so they don't. Expectations live in people's heads instead of in writing, so everyone guesses.
  • A small group ends up absorbing all the work and all the pressure. That holds for a while, until someone burns out, and then the whole thing stalls.

reframe

A Socio-Technical System

→ Open source is a socio-technical system
→ The code and the community are one system, not two
→ Community design decisions matter as much as technical ones
  • So I want to reframe how we look at this. Open source is a socio-technical system. The code and the community are not two separate things you manage separately. They're one system.
  • That means your community design decisions matter just as much as your technical ones.
  • Next: what that looks like when it goes wrong.
problem

The Maintainer Trap

→ Responsibility concentrates instead of distributing
→ Heroic maintainers feel good, until they don't
→ Single points of failure are a community design flaw, not a personality flaw

Example: Rust

Topic-focused teams (compiler, language, libraries, tooling, infrastructure) each own their area independently. A leadership council coordinates across teams. Ownership is spread by design, so no single person carries the whole project.

  • This leads to what I call the maintainer trap. Responsibility concentrates instead of spreading out. We celebrate the heroic maintainer who carries everything, and it feels great, right up until it doesn't.
  • When one person becomes a single point of failure, that's a design flaw in the community, not a weakness in that person.
  • Rust is a nice example of designing against this. Instead of one or two heroes, the project is run by topic-focused teams. Each team owns its own area and sends a representative to a leadership council that looks after the project as a whole. Ownership is spread out by design.

Four Levers That Decide Whether a Project Lasts

Governance

Who decides, and how. Make decision-making explicit before you need it.

Contributor Experience

How easy and rewarding it is to help. Lower the cost of showing up.

Recognition

Whether people's work is seen and valued. Most community labor is invisible.

Local Community

Where belonging actually forms. Global projects still need local homes.

  • If burnout is structural, then we can design against it. Here are four levers that decide whether a project lasts.
  • Governance: who decides and how. Contributor experience: how easy and rewarding it is to help. Recognition: whether the work people do actually gets seen and valued. Local community: where people feel like they belong.
  • We'll walk through each one, with an example of a community that does it well.
  • Next: Lever 1, Governance.
lever 1

Governance

→ Make decision-making explicit before you're in a crisis
→ Shared functions (funding, events, infrastructure) under a neutral body, not individuals
→ Define maintainer roles so authority is distributed, not concentrated
→ Give conflict a defined path, so disputes don't become flame wars
example

Apache Software Foundation

Project management committees elect committers based on merit. Individuals represent themselves, not employers. Decisions happen in the open on mailing lists: if it wasn't recorded, it didn't happen.

Governance is boring until the day you desperately need it.

  • The point of governance is to make decision-making explicit before you're in a crisis. Put the shared functions under a neutral body so they don't land on individual coders. Define maintainer roles so authority is spread out. Give conflict an actual path to resolution.
  • Apache is a good model. Each project is run by a committee that votes on releases and elects new committers based on merit. People participate as individuals, not as employer representatives, so no single company can buy control of a project's direction.
  • Governance is boring until the day you desperately need it.
  • Next: Lever 2, Contributor Experience.
lever 2

Contributor Experience

→ Lower the cost of a first contribution
→ Give newcomers a clear, structured place to start
→ Make the path from first contribution to trusted contributor visible
→ When contributing is confusing, people leave quietly. You never know why.
example

Kubernetes

Published contributor ladder: member to reviewer to approver, with clear expectations at each step. Good first issues. Mentoring cohorts. A graceful way to step back when life gets busy.

Many people enter through a person, not a process. Both matter.

  • The goal is to lower the cost of someone's first contribution. Give newcomers a structured place to start instead of dropping them in front of a repo to figure out where they fit. Make the path from first contribution to trusted contributor visible.
  • Kubernetes does this well. They publish an actual contributor ladder, where you move from member to reviewer to approver, and each step has clear expectations. They tag good first issues, run mentoring cohorts, and have a graceful way for people to step back when life gets busy.
  • When contributing is confusing, people don't complain. They quietly leave, and you never find out why.
  • A personal note: I came in through a person, not a process. Someone experienced reached out and pulled me in before I really understood the project. A lot of people enter that way. Both matter.
  • Next: Lever 3, Recognition.
lever 3

Recognition

→ Most community labor is invisible: reviews, triage, docs, mentoring
→ Invisible work becomes undervalued work
→ Record who actually did what, and let credit extend to the companies that fund that work
example

Drupal Contribution Credit System

Records contribution at scale. Individuals get recognition tied to real work. Companies get visible standing for the work they fund. Commercial incentives align with community health.

  • Most of the labor in a community is invisible. Code review, triage, documentation, mentoring. None of it shows up the way a flashy feature does. And invisible work becomes undervalued work.
  • The fix is to make contribution visible by actually recording who did what, and to let that recognition extend to the companies sponsoring the work, not just individuals.
  • Drupal is a good example, with a contribution credit system that records this at scale.
  • Next: why recognition actually changes behavior.
lever 3

Why Recognition Changes Behavior

→ Individuals get recognition tied to real contribution, not just visibility or self-promotion
→ Companies get visible standing for the work they fund, aligning commercial incentives with community health
→ Recognition becomes a renewable resource, not a favor

When a company treats contribution as part of the actual work, with real time and budget behind it, the alignment becomes real and it lasts. Good incentive design beats good intentions every time.

  • Here's why that recognition changes behavior. Individuals get recognition tied to real contribution, not just visibility or self-promotion. Companies get visible standing for the work they fund, which means their commercial interest lines up with the health of the community.
  • Recognition stops being a favor you do for people and becomes a renewable resource the system produces.
  • The pattern I've seen hold up: when a company treats contribution as part of the actual work, with real time and budget behind it, instead of a side hobby people squeeze in after hours, the alignment becomes real and it lasts.
  • Good incentive design beats good intentions every time.
  • Next: Lever 4, Local Community.
lever 4

Local Community

→ Global projects still need local belonging
→ Pair big global gatherings with small local ones
→ Local events lower the barrier to showing up and meeting people
→ "Come for the code, stay for the community" is a design goal, not a slogan
example

WordPress

A few flagship WordCamps, but hundreds of local volunteer-run events, often just a few dollars to attend. Small and accessible by design. That's where new people get pulled in and where most new organizers learn the ropes.

People commit to projects through other people, not through repositories.

  • Even a global project needs places where people feel they belong locally. The pattern that works is pairing the big global gathering with small local ones. Local events lower the barrier to just showing up and meeting people.
  • WordPress is a great example at scale. They have large flagship WordCamps, but the heart of it is hundreds of local volunteer-run events, often very low-cost. Those events are deliberately kept small and accessible, because that's where new people actually get pulled in and where most new organizers learn the ropes.
  • Come for the code, stay for the community. That's not a slogan, it's a design goal. People commit to projects through other people, not through repositories.

priorities

What to Do Now vs Later

optimize early

Clear contribution paths and onboarding

Explicit, written expectations

Distributing authority before the load gets heavy

Cheap now. Expensive to fix later.

safe to delay

Heavy formal governance and elaborate process

Complex tooling and automation

Over-structuring a community that is still small

Heavy process on a tiny project can strangle it before it grows.

  • Not everything deserves your attention at once. Clear contribution paths, written expectations, and distributing authority are worth doing early. These are cheap now and expensive to fix later.
  • Heavy formal governance, complex tooling, and over-structuring a community that's still small can safely wait.
  • Putting heavy process on a tiny project is its own kind of failure. You can strangle something before it ever gets a chance to grow.

nuance

Good Advice Needs the Right Partner

"Be welcoming to everyone"

Works when paired with triage, so maintainers can keep up.

"Move fast"

Works when paired with shared decision rights, so people stay aligned.

"Document everything"

Works when paired with clear ownership, so the docs stay current.

Context decides whether a practice helps. The practices aren't the problem; the pairing is.

  • One nuance about best practices. The same advice can help or hurt depending on what you pair it with.
  • Be welcoming to everyone is great, as long as it comes with triage. Move fast is great, as long as it comes with shared decision rights. Document everything is great, as long as it comes with clear ownership.
  • The practices themselves aren't the problem. The lesson is to pair each one with the thing that makes it work at scale.

the ai question

Help and Hazard

where ai helps
→ Lowers the barrier to a first contribution
→ Speeds up docs, translation, and issue triage
→ Could ease the "privilege of free time" open source has always depended on
where ai strains
→ A flood of low-effort contributions can overwhelm reviewers
→ The cost shifts from author to maintainer
→ Access is unequal. AI can become a new privilege.

AI doesn't replace community design. It raises the stakes on it. Share the cost and skill of using AI well. Build it into the contributor experience. The same levers still apply.

  • On the good side, AI lowers the barrier to a first contribution. It helps newcomers navigate an unfamiliar codebase, speeds up docs, translation, and triage, and supports people who don't write English as a first language.
  • There's a deeper promise: Dries Buytaert put it well recently, AI can compress the time it takes to understand a codebase enough to act, so it could ease the privilege of free time that open source has always quietly depended on.
  • But AI access isn't equal. The best tools cost real money and real skill. If we want AI to reduce privilege rather than reinforce it, projects have to share two things: the cost, by making lower-cost and open-weight models practical, and the skill, by building AI know-how into the contributor experience.
  • And the strain is real: it's cheap to generate a change, but a maintainer still has to understand it. The test is simple: does AI help more people get from an issue to a tested patch, while making it easier for maintainers to trust and merge?
  • AI doesn't replace community design. It raises the stakes on the same levers: clear paths, recognition for real understanding, and governance that prevents bottlenecks.

mental model

Thrive vs Decay

thriving projects

Share decisions

Make it easy to help

Recognize the work

Build local belonging

decaying projects

Concentrate decisions

Leave contributors guessing

Let invisible work go unseen

Stay purely global

The drift is slow and quiet. That's exactly why it's easy to miss.

  • If you take one mental model from this talk, take this one. It maps straight onto the four levers.
  • Thriving projects share decisions, make it easy to help, recognize the work people do, and build local belonging. Projects that drift do the opposite: decisions pile onto a few people, contributors are left guessing, invisible work goes unseen, and the project never feels local to anyone.
  • The hard part is that the drift is slow and quiet. There's no alarm. That's exactly why it's so easy to miss until it's far along.

your turn

What You Can Realistically Influence

You don't need to become a full-time maintainer.

→ Document one unclear process
→ Review someone else's first contribution
→ Credit invisible work out loud in a channel where people see it
→ Help one local event happen

These are small structural acts. They compound.

  • You might be thinking this is a lot, and you don't want to become a full-time maintainer. Good news: you don't have to.
  • You can document one unclear process. Review someone's first contribution. Credit invisible work out loud in a channel where people see it. Help one local event happen. These are small structural acts, and they compound.
  • That's roughly my own story. I started by volunteering at one event in 2023, then said yes to a few more organizing committees, and that eventually led to me winning the Women in Drupal Award. None of it was a master plan. It was just one small yes at a time.
